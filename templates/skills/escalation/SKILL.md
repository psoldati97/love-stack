# Escalation

When activated, help the user draft a message or conversation plan for a situation that requires pushing back, disagreeing, renegotiating, or escalating.

## The Framework

Every escalation message should have four components:

1. **Acknowledge**: State you understand their position or the current state without conceding.
2. **Reframe**: Name the underlying issue, constraint, or principle the other party may not have considered.
3. **Propose**: Offer a specific alternative, ask, or next step.
4. **Respect the relationship**: Close in a way that preserves future collaboration.

## Escalation Levels

Calibrate tone to the situation. Ask the user which level applies if unclear:

- **Level 1 (Low stakes, first attempt)**: Warm, collaborative, "I might be missing something" framing.
- **Level 2 (Mid stakes, second attempt)**: Direct but diplomatic. Acknowledge the prior exchange. State the disconnect clearly.
- **Level 3 (High stakes, final attempt)**: Formal. State the consequences of continued impasse. Propose a specific path forward or a specific exit.
- **Level 4 (Formal escalation)**: Document-the-record tone. Cc the right people. State the issue in terms of policy, contract, or principle, not personal grievance.

## What to Avoid

- Ultimatums without a real alternative
- Personal attacks or character framing
- Passive aggression ("per my last email", "as discussed")
- Apologies that weaken the position ("sorry to bother")
- Hedging that obscures the ask

## Output

Produce:
- The message itself, at the calibrated level
- One-line summary of the strategic intent
- Optional: a "if they push back" follow-up draft

## When to Invoke

Invoke when the user says: "help me push back", "I need to escalate this", "write a disagreement email", "how do I tell them no", "I want to renegotiate", or describes a professional conflict they need to respond to.
